# Research Brief: Genealogy / Family History

## Metadata
- **Created:** 2024-12-17
- **Query Type:** Genealogical research
- **Version:** 1

## Research Goal

Find newspaper articles that **mention specific people, families, or lineages** - birth/death notices, marriage announcements, family histories, obituaries, and any references that help establish relationships, dates, and life events.

## Relevance Criteria

### Tier 1 - Primary Relevance (INCLUDE)
- Article focuses on a person or family
- Obituaries, birth announcements, marriage notices
- Biographical sketches or profiles
- Genealogical columns (mo'oku'auhau)
- Legal notices mentioning family relationships
- Examples: "He make ana" (death notice), family histories

### Tier 2 - Secondary Relevance (INCLUDE WITH CAVEAT)
- Person mentioned with useful context (role, location, relationships)
- Lists of people (meeting attendees, petition signers, contributors)
- Person referenced in news about events they participated in
- Note the limited genealogical content in summary
- Examples: News article mentioning "J. Kaha of Waimea attended..."

### Tier 3 - Peripheral (EXCLUDE)
- Name appears only as author attribution
- Person mentioned without any biographical context
- Name appears in advertisement without personal details
- Examples: "Letter to editor from J. Kaha" (unless content reveals biographical info)

**Note:** Genealogical research has DIFFERENT relevance criteria than topic research. A "passing mention" of a person may still be valuable if it establishes their existence, location, or time period.

## Extraction Priorities

When reading relevant articles, capture these details in order of importance:

1. **Full names** - Hawaiian names, Christian names, nicknames
2. **Relationships** - Parents, children, spouses, siblings (keiki, makua, etc.)
3. **Dates** - Birth, death, marriage, events
4. **Places** - Residence, birth place, burial location
5. **Roles/occupations** - What they did, positions held
6. **Associated names** - Other people mentioned in connection
7. **Source context** - Who wrote this, what was the occasion

## Bias Awareness

Patterns that might skew results for this research type:

- **Elite/chiefly bias:** Ali'i and prominent families have more newspaper coverage than maka'āinana (commoners). Absence of records doesn't mean absence of people.

- **Christian name bias:** People with Western/Christian names may be easier to search than those with Hawaiian names only.

- **Geographic bias:** Some regions had better newspaper coverage. Rural and remote areas may be underrepresented.

- **Gender bias:** Men's activities were more frequently reported than women's. Women often appear only in relation to male family members.

- **Literacy/participation bias:** Families who engaged with newspapers (subscribers, letter writers) are overrepresented.

## Search Strategy

### Primary Terms
- Full name variations: "Kalākaua", "David Kalākaua", "D. Kalākaua"
- Family name alone: may return many results, useful for discovering family members
- Place + name: "Kaha o Waimea" to narrow results

### Secondary Terms
Terms that surface genealogical content:
- make (death), hanau (birth), mare (marry/marriage)
- mo'oku'auhau (genealogy)
- ohana (family)
- keiki (child), makua (parent)

### Term Combinations
Effective patterns:
- [name] + [place]: "Kaha Kohala"
- [family event] + [name]: "make ana Kaha"
- [relationship] + [name]: "keiki a Kaha"

## Notes

### Name Variations
Hawaiian names may appear with or without diacriticals, and with various spellings:
- Search multiple variations
- OCR may render names inconsistently
- 'Okina may appear as apostrophe, backtick, or be missing

### Connecting Records
A single article may not provide complete information. Value comes from:
- Cross-referencing multiple mentions
- Building timeline from scattered references
- Connecting names that appear together

### Legal and Official Notices
Check for:
- Land records (mahele, kuleana)
- Court notices
- Government appointments
- Naturalization records

These often contain precise names, dates, and relationships.

### Respecting Living Descendants
When research may connect to living families, consider:
- How findings will be shared
- Sensitivity around some historical records
- Connecting with descendants before publication
