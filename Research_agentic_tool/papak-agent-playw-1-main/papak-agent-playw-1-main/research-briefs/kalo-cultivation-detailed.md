# Research Brief: Kalo Cultivation (Detailed)

## Metadata
- **Created:** 2024-12-17
- **Query Type:** Cultural practices - Agricultural (specialized)
- **Extends:** cultural-practices.md
- **Version:** 1

## Research Goal

Find newspaper articles about **kalo/taro farming and cultivation** with emphasis on:
- Named taro varieties (huli types)
- Region-specific practices
- Soil preparation and nutrition techniques

Prioritize articles with **specific, actionable details** over general mentions.

## Relevance Criteria

### Tier 1 - HIGH VALUE (Include, prioritize in results)
Articles that contain ANY of:
- **Named taro varieties** (e.g., Lehua, Moi, Huli-Pake) - even partial lists
- **Specific regional practices** (e.g., "in Waipi'o they do X", "Hanalei method")
- **Soil/nutrition techniques** (fertilizers, soil preparation, amendments)
- Detailed planting, water management, or harvesting methods

### Tier 1 - MODERATE VALUE (Include)
- General cultivation advice without regional/variety specifics
- Comparison of wet (lo'i) vs dry (malo'o) methods
- Discussions of kalo farming as an industry/practice
- Historical accounts of cultivation without technical details

### Tier 3 - LOW VALUE (Exclude unless notable)
- Mentions kalo as one of several crops without cultivation focus
- Exhortations to plant kalo without explaining how
- Kalo mentioned only as food/poi, not farming
- Economic/market discussions without growing information

## Extraction Priorities

When reading relevant articles, capture in this order:

1. **Variety names** - ONLY list names you can read with confidence; note how many others appear unreadable
2. **Regional information** - Which areas, what local variations or recommendations
3. **Soil/nutrition details** - Fertilizers, soil types, preparation methods, amendments
4. **Water management** - Lo'i construction, water flow, irrigation
5. **Planting techniques** - Huli selection, spacing, timing, seasons
6. **Pest/disease management** - Problems and solutions mentioned
7. **Author/source type** - Note if traditional practitioner vs institutional source

## Source Weighting

**Prefer traditional knowledge sources** when available:
- Articles by named Hawaiian practitioners
- Regional correspondents describing local methods
- Mo'olelo or narratives about cultivation practices
- Letters describing family or community practices

**Institutional sources acceptable** (government, experiment stations) when:
- Traditional sources not available for a topic
- They provide unique specific details (measurements, experiments)
- Note the source type in summary so researchers can assess

**When presenting results:** Lead with traditional knowledge sources where possible; group institutional sources separately or note their origin clearly.

## Bias Awareness

- **Source type**: Search results may favor institutional/government articles - actively look for practitioner voices
- **Regional coverage**: Major valleys may have more coverage than remote areas
- **Time period**: 1900s articles often more explicit; 1800s may assume baseline knowledge
- **OCR quality**: Variety name lists are highly vulnerable to OCR errors

## Search Strategy

### Primary Terms (High Yield)
- "kanu kalo" - planting taro
- "mahi kalo" - cultivating taro
- "huli kalo" - taro tops/planting material
- "loi kalo" - taro pond field
- "kalo maloo" - dryland taro

### Soil/Nutrition Specific
- "lepo kalo" - taro soil
- "momona" - fertile/rich (soil context)
- "puna" - lime (soil amendment)
- "mea hoomomona" - fertilizer

### Traditional Knowledge Terms
- "loina kanu kalo" - traditions of planting kalo
- "na kupuna" - the ancestors (may surface traditional methods)
- "wa kahiko" - ancient times

### Regional Searches
- "kalo [region]" - for any specific region of interest
- All regions treated equally; specificity valued over location

### Variety-Focused
- "ano kalo" - types of taro
- "huli" + context about varieties

## OCR Handling for Varieties

**Strict confidence rule for variety names:**
- ONLY report names you can read with full confidence
- Do NOT guess, infer, or "clean up" garbled text
- Note quantity: "Article lists approximately 15 variety names; clearly readable: Huli-Pake, Lehua; remainder not legible due to OCR quality"
- Recommend original scan review when variety lists present but unreadable

## Notes

This brief prioritizes **specificity over breadth**. An article with detailed soil advice for one region is more valuable than a general overview. When ranking results:
1. Lead with articles containing specific techniques, varieties, or regional details
2. Prefer traditional practitioner sources over institutional
3. Note source type so researchers can weight accordingly
