## Summary

This research prioritized:
1. Growing methods and planting techniques
2. Soil preparation and nutrition requirements
3. Regional cultivation practices
4. Varietals only when connected to farming/soil information

## Key Findings

### Challenge: Awa vs Awa (Plant vs Harbor)

"Awa" in Hawaiian means both the kava plant AND harbor/channel. This created significant noise in search results, requiring careful filtering. Additionally, most newspaper articles about awa (the plant) focus on:
- Social/moral concerns about drinking
- Legislative/legal discussions
- Commercial trade/sales
- Ritual/ceremonial uses

**Cultivation-specific information is relatively rare** compared to kalo research.

## Article Details

### Tier 1 - HIGH VALUE

#### 1. He Moolelo No Ka Awa A Me Kona Wahi I Loaa Mai Ai (1930)

**"A Story About Awa and Where It Came From"**

- **Source:** Ke Alakai o Hawaii, August 14, 1930
- **Author:** John Maka
- **URL:** https://www.papakilodatabase.com/pdnupepa/?a=d&d=KAHEEL19300814-01.2.23
- **Raw text:** `raw-articles/KAHEEL19300814-01.2.23_2025-12-18.txt`

**Key Content:**

**Propagation Method ("O Kona Kumu E Ulu Ai"):**
- Awa is propagated from cuttings/branches (lala)
- Method: When harvesting awa by pulling (uhuki), break off a branch and throw it into the hole ("lua opu awa") where awa was pulled
- Plant firmly ("kanu a paa") and it begins growing ("omaku ae")
- Roots spread and crawl ("kolo ke aa")

**Variety Names Mentioned:**
- Papa
- Alikea
- Mokihana
- Notes: "he keokeo ko lakou lala" (white stems/branches)
- "puawa" - offspring/sprouts of different sizes

**Growing Regions:**
- Kamaile, Kauai (mountain of Waialeale mentioned in origin story)
- Halawa, Oahu
- Puna, Hawaii (noted with some uncertainty)
- Hakipuu, Oahu (famous place for awa - "wahi pana")

**Traditional Preparation/Uses:**
- Used with puaa hiwa (black pig) for medicinal purposes
- Ritual preparation with prayer to aumakua
- Leaves (lau) used for medicinal purposes - "haliiliiia ka lau malalo" (leaves laid underneath)

**Note:** Article contains origin story (mo'olelo) connecting awa to Moikeha and the woman Ewa. The name "awa" comes from the intoxicating effect ("ona") experienced when first consumed.

#### 2. KA MOOLELO HAWAII, Helu 8 (1869)

**"The Hawaiian History, Chapter 8" — Kamakau's Comprehensive Awa Cultivation Account**

- **Source:** Ke Au Okoa, December 2, 1869
- **Author:** S. M. Kamakau
- **URL:** https://www.papakilodatabase.com/pdnupepa/?a=d&d=KAO18691202-01.2.2
- **Raw text:** `raw-articles/KAO18691202-01.2.2_2026-01-29.txt`
- **Series:** Yes (part of Ka Moolelo Hawaii; continues across issues)

**Key Content:**

**Varieties with Physical Descriptions:**
- Papa eleele, papakoa - tall/proud growth ("nenee haaheo")
- Hiwa, moi - reddish joints like honuoula ("ponapona ine ka puna ko honuoula")
- Makea - hakai like hair, bluish ("hauliuli")
- Mokihana - hakai like bamboo belly ("opu ohe"), clustered branching
- General: round leaves ("lau pohepohe"), attractive joints ("ponapona akaaka")

**Growing Conditions:**
- Rainy lands ("aina ua nui") and warm lands ("aina mehana")
- Moist slopes that channel water ("makaili hanono wai")
- Forest edges ("lae laau") and kukaepuaa
- Sheltered/consistent areas ("omau") with rain

**Planting Method (Detailed):**
1. Farmer goes to the shore ("kahakai") to collect organic material (hailini)
2. Carries material to the planting site
3. Carefully protects the node/joint of the cutting ("puupuu o ka akanka")
4. Composts with rich soil ("lepo opilopilo") and organic waste ("opala")
5. Lets material decompose before planting ("kipulu")

**Co-cultivation:**
- Awa co-planted with wauke, dryland kalo, maia (banana), and ko (sugarcane)
- Most awa was planted in small amounts on kuauna (borders/edges) of kalo gardens and wauke plots
- Some planted in dedicated mala (gardens)

**Maturation and Propagation:**
- Awa reaches maturity (oo) in 2-3 years ("elua a ekolu makahiki")
- Propagation via kakiwi (stem cuttings) from the base of the plant
- When puawa (young shoots) emerge, kakiwi are taken to create new plantings

**Famous Regions (from Ancient Times):**
- Koukou on Kauai
- Hena [OCR unclear]
- Lanakila on Maui
- Puna on Hawaii
- Known for quality and potency (ona) of awa

**Significance:** This is the most comprehensive traditional account of awa cultivation found in the newspaper collection. Kamakau provides details found nowhere else: soil preparation using shore-collected material and composting, co-cultivation practices, and the full propagation cycle from kakiwi to puawa. The composting method ("lepo opilopilo a me ka opala") represents a sophisticated soil enrichment technique.

**"Wanting Awa as Plant/Medicine"**

- **Source:** Ka Nupepa Kuokoa, April 14, 1911

- **Raw text:** `raw-articles/KNK19110414-01.2.49_2025-12-18.txt`

**Key Content:**

**Commercial Wild Harvest:**

- Hundreds of tons ("he mau haaeri ka nui o na tona awa") available

- Workers paid $135/ton to harvest

**Growing Habitat:**

- Harvested at Awini
- Transported to Mahukona
- Shipped to Hilo
- Exported to New York

**Note:** This article documents wild harvest, not cultivation. However, it provides valuable information about natural awa habitat (mountain elevations in Kohala).

#### 3. NO KA AWA. OLELO AE LIKE (1846)

**"About Awa. Agreement" — The 1846 Awa Prohibition Law**

- **Source:** Ka Elele, October 21, 1846
- **URL:** https://www.papakilodatabase.com/pdnupepa/?a=d&d=KE18461021-01.2.4
- **Raw text:** `raw-articles/KE18461021-01.2.4_2026-01-22.txt`

**Key Content:**

**Legal Framework for Awa Cultivation:**
- Signed by Kamehameha, Keoni Ana (August 6, 1846)
- One mala (garden) permitted per island: Hawaii, Maui, Oahu, Kauai
- Maximum garden size: 6 acres ("eono eka")
- Gardens permitted only for medicinal use (lapaau)
- All illegally-grown awa can be pulled by anyone

**Significance:** This is the actual legislative text regulating awa farming. The 6-acre maximum implies pre-existing gardens were at least that large, and the per-island regulation shows awa was widely cultivated on all major islands.

**"The Debate About Awa" — Economic Data on Awa Farming**

- **Source:** Ka Elele, February 17, 1847
- **Author:** J. H. Kaehu
- **URL:** https://www.papakilodatabase.com/pdnupepa/?a=d&d=KE18470217-01.2.7
- **Raw text:** `raw-articles/KE18470217-01.2.7_2026-01-22.txt`

**Garden Economics:**

- Specific dimensions: "elua eka ka loa; hookahi eka ka laula" (2 eka long, 1 eka wide)

**Context:** Written as a dialogue between "Inuawa" (Awa-drinker, arguing for cultivation) and "Kinaiawa" (Awa-prohibitionist). Provides the only quantitative economic data about awa farming in the newspaper collection.

#### 5. HE WAHI MANAO PAIPAI (1849)

**"Some Encouraging Thoughts" — Awa Regrowth in Kohala Gardens**

- **Source:** Ka Elele, May 19, 1849
- **Author:** J. H. Kaihalapia (Kohala)
- **URL:** https://www.papakilodatabase.com/pdnupepa/?a=d&d=KE18490519-01.2.4
- **Raw text:** `raw-articles/KE18490519-01.2.4_2026-01-22.txt`

**Key Content:**

**Garden Prevalence:**
- "ua pau ka awa i ka uhukiia ma na mala a pau o Kohala nei" — ALL awa in ALL gardens of Kohala was pulled out
- Enforcement by Governor's order, carried out by Makai (police)

**Natural Regrowth:**
- "Mahope ulu wale hou mai kekahi anau kumu awa ma na mala o kanaka" — Afterward, awa began growing again on its own in people's gardens
- "kumu awa" (awa plants/trunks) regrowing

**Significance:** Demonstrates that awa was universally grown in Kohala gardens (mala), and that the plant is resilient enough to regrow from roots after being pulled out. This regrowth ability corroborates the "kolo ke aa" (crawling roots) described by John Maka.

#### 6. KAPU KA AWA (1847)

**"Awa is Taboo" — Official Planting Ban**

- **Source:** Ka Elele, October 6, 1847
- **Author:** S. W. Maikai, Luna kuai Awa o ka Mokupuni Oahu (Awa Sales Officer for Oahu)
- **URL:** https://www.papakilodatabase.com/pdnupepa/?a=d&d=KE18471006-01.2.14
- **Raw text:** `raw-articles/KE18471006-01.2.14_2026-01-22.txt`

**Key Content:**

- "aole oukou e kanu iki i ka awa" — You shall not plant any awa at all
- Awa planted after August 6, 1846 to be pulled out ("E uhuki wale ia kela awa")
- Mentions "kahuna lapaau haole" (foreign doctors) accepting awa as medicinal
- Prohibition on secret selling

**Significance:** The existence of a dedicated "Luna kuai Awa" (Awa Sales Officer) for each island demonstrates the scale of the awa trade and cultivation. The ban on planting confirms people were actively cultivating awa, not just harvesting wild.

#### 11. HE KANAWAI - E hoololi ana no ka awa (1856)

**"A Law - Amending [the statute] regarding awa" — Legalization of Awa Planting**

- **Source:** Ka Hae Hawaii, October 1, 1856
- **URL:** https://www.papakilodatabase.com/pdnupepa/?a=d&d=KHH18561001-01.2.18
- **Raw text:** `raw-articles/KHH18561001-01.2.18_2026-01-29.txt`

**Key Content:**

**Legal Framework for Cultivation:**
- Pauku 1 explicitly legalizes planting: "e hiki no ma ke kanawai, i na kanaka a pau ko kanu i ka awa maloko o keia Aupuni" (all people may plant awa in this Kingdom)
- Establishes Luna kuai awa (licensed awa sellers)
- Consumption restricted to medicinal use with kahuna lapaau prescription

**Economic Structure:**
- Pricing: "elua hapakolu e haawiia ika mea nana ka awa, a hookahi hapakolu o Ilo no ke Aupuni" (2/3 to the grower, 1/3 to the government)

**Significance:** This 1854 law (published 1856) reversed the 1846 prohibition. It demonstrates that the government recognized awa farming as an economic activity worth regulating rather than banning, with a formal revenue-sharing model between growers and government.

#### 12. No ka awa (1871)

**"About Awa" — Awa as Traditional Cultivated Plant**

- **Source:** Ke Au Okoa, April 13, 1871
- **URL:** https://www.papakilodatabase.com/pdnupepa/?a=d&d=KAO18710413-01.2.22
- **Raw text:** `raw-articles/KAO18710413-01.2.22_2026-01-29.txt`

**Key Content:**

- Awa was intentionally cultivated ("hooulu nui ia") across many Pacific island groups, not just gathered wild
- Awa was not distilled/processed like foreign spirits ("wai ikaika"); prepared directly from the root ("lepo ka awa"), chewed ("mumi ia") and drunk
- Preparation takes weeks ("mau pule")
- Many people actively planting awa: "he nui no ka poe e kanu ana"

**Significance:** An advocacy piece arguing against awa prohibition. Provides context that awa was a cultivated plant across the Pacific, not a wild-harvested substance, and was important to mahiai (farmers).

#### 13. No Ka Mahiai Ma Hamakua (1857)

**"On Farming at Hamakua" — Agricultural Census with Awa Acreage**

- **Source:** Ka Hae Hawaii, June 17, 1857
- **Author:** N. Eeau, Vice President, Ahahui Mahiai o Hamakua (Agricultural Society of Hamakua)
- **URL:** https://www.papakilodatabase.com/pdnupepa/?a=d&d=KHH18570617-01.2.4
- **Raw text:** `raw-articles/KHH18570617-01.2.4_2026-01-29.txt`

**Key Content:**

**Awa Acreage by District (1857):**
- Kawela: 14 eka (largest) — also grew ipu 31 eka, maia 10, paka 23
- Kapulena: 6 eka — alongside kalo 172, uala 140, wauke 10
- Honokau: 4 eka — alongside kalo 23, uala 25, maia 8
- Kaolie: ~1 eka (OCR unclear)
- **Total Hamakua: 25 eka awa** (with 23,714 kope trees, 935 alani trees)

**Significance:** Only known quantitative agricultural census documenting awa acreage by district. Shows awa was a relatively small crop (25 eka) compared to coffee (23,714 trees) but was grown in multiple Hamakua sub-districts. Kawela had the largest awa cultivation (14 eka). The co-planting data (awa alongside kalo, wauke, maia) is consistent with Kamakau's description of awa as an interplanted crop. This is the earliest post-prohibition agricultural data for awa.

#### 14. HE MOOLELO KAAO NO Namakaokapaoo (1894)

**"A Legend of Namakaokapaoo" — Awa Garden at Waiohuli, Kula (Maui)**

- **Source:** Ka Nupepa Kuokoa, June 9, 1894
- **Author:** John K. Hiihin (Lihue, Kauai)
- **URL:** https://www.papakilodatabase.com/pdnupepa/?a=d&d=KNK18940609-01.2.29
- **Raw text:** `raw-articles/KNK18940609-01.2.29_2026-01-29.txt`
- **Series:** Yes (Namakaokapaoo; continues)

**Key Content:**

**Awa Garden Description:**
- "mala awa" (awa garden) explicitly named as part of a large farm (mahinaai nui) at Waiohuli, Kula, Maui
- Co-cultivated alongside uala (sweet potato), maia (banana), and ko (sugarcane) — consistent with Kamakau's co-cultivation description
- Awa described as tall as trees: "he like pu no ke kiekie o ka awa me ka laau"

**Harvesting:**
- Awa harvested by uhuki (pulling) — consistent with all other sources
- Child warrior destroyed the garden by pulling awa from all sides: "pau loa ika uhukiia mai kela aoao a keia aoao"

**Growing Region:**
- Waiohuli, Kula, on the slopes of Haleakala, Maui
- First Maui-specific awa garden location identified in the newspaper collection

**Significance:** Although a legend (mo'olelo ka'ao), this fills the Maui coverage gap. The agricultural details are incidental to the story and therefore likely reflect real farming practices: awa gardens co-planted with other crops, awa growing as tall as trees, harvesting by pulling. The Waiohuli/Kula location on the slopes of Haleakala is consistent with awa's preference for mountain/upland areas and Kamakau's mention of Lanakila (Maui) as a famous awa region.

#### 15. Halawai o ka ahahui mahiai o Kona Akau (1862)

**"Meeting of the Agricultural Society of North Kona" — Commercial Awa Planting Committee**

- **Source:** Ka Hoku o ka Pakipika, March 6, 1862
- **Author:** G. W. Pilipo
- **URL:** https://www.papakilodatabase.com/pdnupepa/?a=d&d=KHP18620306-01.2.20
- **Raw text:** `raw-articles/KHP18620306-01.2.20_2026-01-29.txt`

**Key Content:**

**Committee Appointment:**
- Vice President appointed a committee ("komite wae") to evaluate: "ke puhi rama, ame ke kanu awa e kuai, ame ke kanu waina a kaomi waina" — distilling rum, planting awa for sale, and planting/pressing wine grapes
- Committee members: Nakapalani, J.W. Makanoanoa, J.H. Kamalo

**Context:**
- Agricultural Society meeting at Kona Akau (North Kona, Hawaii island), February 25, 1862
- Awa grouped with rum and wine as potential commercial agricultural ventures
- This is the only agricultural society report in the collection that explicitly considers awa as a crop

**Significance:** Documents formal institutional consideration of awa as a commercial crop just 6 years after the 1856 legalization law. The committee was tasked with evaluating whether awa planting for sale was viable — a follow-up report from this committee could contain detailed cultivation information. The contrast with other agricultural societies (which focused on tobacco, cotton, rice, and coffee) suggests Kona had particular interest in awa as a cash crop.

### Key Insight: Disambiguation Strategy
The search engine performs OR-matching on multi-word queries. The most effective strategy is to use terms that can ONLY refer to the awa plant in specific contexts:
1. **Harvesting terms** ("uhuki awa") - pulling/uprooting only applies to plants
2. **Variety names** ("awa ililena") - specific cultivar references
3. **Scientific names** ("Piper methysticum") - botanical only
4. **Region + awa** combinations where the region is inland (not a harbor)

## Bias Observations

### Source Type
- Two comprehensive cultivation accounts from traditional practitioners: S. M. Kamakau (1869) and John Maka (1930)
- Kamakau's account is embedded within his broader Ka Moolelo Hawaii series documenting traditional Hawaiian knowledge
- The 1846-1849 prohibition articles are government/institutional but provide the richest quantitative data
- No experiment station or agricultural extension articles about awa exist in the collection
- Traditional cultivation knowledge was likely transmitted orally - the newspapers were largely vehicles for prohibition enforcement and social commentary

### Agricultural Society Bias (Session 5 Finding)
- Searched 10 different Agricultural Society (Ahahui Mahiai) reports from Kau, Hamakua, Waioli/Kauai, Molokai, Lahaina, Hana, Makawao, Kaupo, and Kona Akau
- **9 of 10 had zero awa content.** These societies overwhelmingly focused on cash crops for export: tobacco (papapa), cotton (pulupulu), coffee (kope), rice, and sugar
- Only exception: Kona Akau (1862) appointed a committee to evaluate commercial awa planting
- This confirms that awa was not part of the formal agricultural modernization movement of the 1850s-1860s, despite being a traditional Hawaiian crop. Awa knowledge likely remained in oral/informal channels.

### Medicinal vs Agricultural Focus
- Most awa articles discuss awa as a substance to consume, not as a crop to grow
- Cultivation details appear **incidentally** within prohibition enforcement, debate, and legendary contexts
- The prohibition era (1846-1849) ironically provides the best farming data because enforcement required documenting gardens

### Regional Coverage
- **Well-documented:** Kohala (1849, 1911), Hakipuu/Koolaupoko (1847, 1922, 1930), Puna (1906, 1911, 1930), Hamakua (1857 census)
- **Mentioned but not detailed:** Kalihi, Waialua/Pupukea/Waiahole
- **Partially filled:** Maui (Waiohuli/Kula confirmed via 1894 legend; Kona Akau commercial planting committee 1862)
- **OCR gap:** Two promising Puna articles (KAA19111118, KAA19111202) about "Ka Awa Kau Laau o Puna" have completely unreadable OCR — PDF scans needed

### Time Period Pattern
- **1835-1849:** Prohibition era - richest source of quantitative cultivation data (ironic)
- **1850-1871:** Post-prohibition legislative reconstruction (1856 legalization, 1862 Kona Akau commercial awa committee, 1870 regulation, 1871 advocacy) and Kamakau's systematic documentation (1869)
- **1877-1900:** Continued secret cultivation despite regulation (1877 mountain gardens)
- **1900-1930:** Revival of interest - commercial harvest (1911), traditional knowledge preservation (1930)
- **Modern (Ka Wai Ola):** Cultural restoration context; no farming specifics

## Synthesis: What We Know About Awa Cultivation

### Soil Preparation and Composting (NEW - Kamakau 1869)
- Farmer goes to the shore ("kahakai") to collect organic material ("hailini") - likely seaweed or marine organic matter
- Material is carried to the planting site
- The node/joint of the cutting ("puupuu o ka akanka") is carefully protected
- Composted with rich soil ("lepo opilopilo") and organic waste ("opala")
- Material is allowed to decompose ("kipulu") before planting
- This represents a sophisticated soil enrichment technique using marine-sourced nutrients

### Propagation
- **Kakiwi (stem cuttings):** Cuttings taken from the base of mature plants to start new puawa — Kamakau, 1869
- **Puawa (young shoots):** Emerge from planted kakiwi; become new plants in the propagation cycle — Kamakau, 1869
- **Branch replanting:** When harvesting by pulling (uhuki), break off a branch and replant in the hole — John Maka, 1930
- **Natural regrowth:** Awa regrows from roots after pulling — confirmed in Kohala 1849
- **Maturation:** 2-3 years ("elua a ekolu makahiki") to reach harvest maturity — Kamakau, 1869
- Roots crawl and spread ("kolo ke aa") — consistent across sources

### Growing Conditions (NEW - Kamakau 1869)
- **Rainy lands** ("aina ua nui") — good for awa growth
- **Warm lands** ("aina mehana") — also suitable
- **Moist slopes** ("makaili hanono wai") that channel water
- **Forest edges** ("lae laau") and kukaepuaa
- **Sheltered areas** ("omau") with consistent rainfall

### Co-cultivation (Kamakau 1869, Namakaokapaoo 1894)
- Awa co-planted with wauke, dryland kalo, maia (banana), and ko (sugarcane) — Kamakau
- Most awa planted in small amounts on kuauna (borders/edges) of kalo gardens and wauke plots — Kamakau
- Some dedicated mala (gardens) for awa, but most was interplanted — Kamakau
- Namakaokapaoo legend (1894) independently confirms co-cultivation: mala awa alongside uala, maia, ko on a large farm (mahinaai nui) at Waiohuli, Kula, Maui

### Garden Characteristics
- **Location:** Mountain areas (kuahiwi), cliff-sides (pali), uplands (uka), forest edges (lae laau)
- **Size:** Up to 6 acres (1846 law maximum); 2 acres described as commercially viable (1847); total 25 eka across 5 Hamakua districts (1857 census)
- **Scale comparison:** In 1857 Hamakua, awa (25 eka) was a minor crop vs. kalo (75+ eka at Kaala alone), wauke, and kope (23,714 trees). Kawela had the largest awa cultivation at 14 eka.
- **Economics:** 2-acre garden yields $300+ (1847); wild harvest at $250/ton (1911); grower receives 2/3, government 1/3 under 1856 law
- **Commercial planning:** North Kona Agricultural Society (1862) formally evaluated "ke kanu awa e kuai" (planting awa for sale) as a commercial venture alongside rum and wine
- **Management:** Guards posted at chiefly gardens (1935 legend); government permits for medicinal harvesting (1847)

### Harvesting and Preparation
- Pulled by hand (uhuki/huhuki) — consistent across all sources
- Kumu (trunk/base) and aa (roots) are the parts used
- Prepared directly from the root ("lepo ka awa"), chewed ("mumi ia") — not distilled or processed like foreign spirits (1871)
- Preparation takes weeks ("mau pule") — 1871

### Habitat
- Mountain forests, especially with ohia trees (Ka-Miki legend)
- Cliff-side locations at Waipio (Pupualenalena legend)
- Slopes of Haleakala at Waiohuli/Kula, Maui — awa as tall as trees (Namakaokapaoo legend 1894)
- Upland areas across all islands; cultivated across the Pacific (1871)
- Resilient: regrows after being pulled out
