## Article Details

### 1. Na Loina Kanu Kalo a na Kupuna Hawaii i ke Au Kahiko (1922)

**"Traditional Kalo Planting Practices of Hawaiian Ancestors in the Old Days"**

- **Source:** Ka Nupepa Kuokoa, April 21, 1922
- **URL:** https://www.papakilodatabase.com/pdnupepa/?a=d&d=KNK19220421-01.2.14
- **Raw text:** `raw-articles/KNK19220421-01.2.14_2025-12-18.txt`
- **Series:** Yes - "(Aole i pau)" - continues in next issue

**Key Content:**

**Two Cultivation Types:**
- Kanu lo'i (wetland/pond field) - most common outside Hawaii Island & Maui
- Kanu maloo (dryland) - for areas without flowing water

**Soil Management Practices:**
- After harvest, turn nahelehele (weeds) and leaves of hau and milo into soil as fertilizer
- Hoomaloo (dry out) the lo'i after harvest to:
  - Allow sun to remove awaawa (sourness/acidity) from soil
  - Let organic matter decompose before replanting
- Traditional practice: dry after every two harvests

**Disease Treatment (for popo/rot):**
1. Hoomaloo ka lo'i (dry the field)
2. Hoohuli i ka lepo (turn the soil) - by paeli or palau
3. Let rest for several months
4. Add puna keokeo (white lime)
5. Hookomo i ka wai (flood with water)
6. Wait several weeks
7. Turn again, then plant huli

**Weeding Practices:**
- Weed twice before harvest (elua manawa)
- Move carefully to avoid cutting roots (aa)
- Don't weed if kalo almost ready (kokoke e oo) - disturbing causes problems

**Note:** Article explicitly states that traditional knowledge (ike kanu kalo a na kupuna) had been lost and the author is documenting ancestral practices.

### 3. Papa Inoa O Na Kalo Like Ole A Me Ko Lakou Ano (1918)

**"List of Different Taro Types and Their Characteristics"**

- **Source:** Ka Hoku o Hawaii, August 8, 1918
- **Author:** Z.P. Kalokuokamaile, Napoopoo (Kona)
- **URL:** https://www.papakilodatabase.com/pdnupepa/?a=d&d=KHHA19180808-01.2.20
- **Raw text:** `raw-articles/KHHA19180808-01.2.20_2025-12-18.txt`
- **Source Type:** TRADITIONAL - Kona practitioner

**Regional Information:**
- Specifically documents "na kalo o Kona nei i ka wa kahiko" (taro of Kona in the old times)
- States approximately 60 varieties from Kona: "e piha ke kanaono kalo"
- Notes some varieties were abandoned due to slow-growing i'o (corm)

**Variety Names with Descriptions (Selected Highlights):**

| Variety | Description (as readable in OCR) |
|---------|----------------------------------|
| Apuwai | Hakeakea (pale) huli, leaf shaped like heart, water floats on top |
| Apii | Hakeakea huli like Lehua, leaf is pupuka a apiipii (wrinkled) |
| Aweu | Related to Ulaula, Lauloa, Palakea families; thrives in forest, doesn't die in sun |
| Elepaio | Hakeakea huli, leaf has kikokiko keokeo (white speckles) |
| Ieie | Like Kumu with ula weo (red); flower is red like ieie plant |
| Lehua | Hakeakea huli, grows large like Lauloa |
| Makoko | Like Ohe ula; poi is more red than other varieties |
| Uahiapele | Eleele (dark) huli like Naiowea; leaf edge haula, shiny like volcanic haze |
| Uia | Like Manini with stripes; corm has aala (fragrance) |

**Lost Varieties Noted:** Several varieties marked "aole ike ia" (not known/not seen) - indicating knowledge loss even by 1918:
- Ulanui, Hinapu, Hinale, Kukaeiole, Ko-ae, Manauea, Nohu, Nina, Nao, Paua, Piko, Wolu

**OCR Quality Note:** Detailed descriptions have moderate OCR noise. Raw text preserved for verification. Recommend original scan review for variety-description pairs.

### 5. No Ka Hooulu Ana I Ke Kalo (1911) - Institutional Source

**"About Growing Taro"**

- **Source:** Ke Aloha Aina, July 1, 1911
- **Authors:** E.V. Wilcox (Agent, US Dept of Agriculture) & F.A. Clowes (Manager, Small Farm Stations of Hawaii)
- **URL:** https://www.papakilodatabase.com/pdnupepa/?a=d&d=KAA19110701-01.2.18
- **Raw text:** `raw-articles/KAA19110701-01.2.18_2025-12-18.txt`
- **Source Type:** INSTITUTIONAL - Agricultural Experiment Station
- **Series:** Yes - "(Ka Hopena)" indicates conclusion; earlier parts exist

**Key Content:**

**Variety Count:**
- Approximately 60 varieties in Hawaii ("ma kahi o 60 ano kalo o Hawaii nei")
- Notes naming confusion across islands - same variety may have different names on different islands

**Varieties Mentioned:**
> Piialii, Lehua eleele, Lehua keokeo, Kieeli, Palaii, Kuohpo, Wehiwa, Apii kea, Kumu, Elepaio, Moi, Lauloa, Uaua, Mana

**Disease Information:**
- **Popo** (rot) - can be avoided with good huli; replant after one year if affected
- **Ponalo** - "poko lele" type disease affecting kalo
- Treatment: use puna (lime) to dry out soil

**Classification Notes:**
- Kalo classified by: growth habits, water requirements, eating quality, time to maturity
- Mana variety noted for its i'o (corm) quality
- Piialii described as most delicious poi

**Note:** This is the final installment of a series. Earlier parts (June 10, June 17, June 23, June 30, 1911) likely contain additional cultivation details.
