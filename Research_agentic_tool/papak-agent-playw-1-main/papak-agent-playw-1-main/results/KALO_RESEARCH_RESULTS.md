# Kalo (Taro) Cultivation Research Results

## Research Query
Find newspaper articles related to growing Kalo or taro, ideally with farming advice. Specifically looking for articles that give historical advice about growing kalo in particular regions.

## Search Strategy
1. Searched Hawaiian Newspapers Collection at papakilodatabase.com
2. Used multiple search terms: "kanu kalo", "Na Loina Kanu Kalo", "hooulu kalo", "mahi kalo", "loi kalo", "kuhikuhi kalo"
3. Filtered for articles with agricultural/farming content
4. Looked for regional-specific advice

## Key Articles Found

### 1. He mau kuhikuhi no na me e pili ana i ke Kalo
**"Some instructions/guidelines for things related to Kalo"**

- **Source:** Ka Nupepa Kuokoa, Volume XLI, Number 13
- **Date:** March 27, 1903
- **Author:** T. P. Sedgwick (Hawaii Agricultural Experiment Station)
- **URL:** https://www.papakilodatabase.com/pdnupepa/?a=d&d=KNK19030327-01.2.34

**Content Summary:**
- Root rot (pala) prevention experiments conducted at Kalihi
- Water management techniques for loʻi
- Specific fertilizer recommendations:
  - Puna (lime): 4 tons per acre, lasts 5 years
  - Nitrate of soda: 160-180 pounds per acre
  - Phosphoric acid, potash, ammonia ratios
- Timing for fertilizer application (2-3 months after planting huli)
- Soil preparation before planting

### 2. KA OIHANA KANU KALO
**"The Work/Business of Planting Taro"**

- **Source:** Ka Hoku o Hawaii, Volume XV, Number 32
- **Date:** January 5, 1922
- **URL:** https://www.papakilodatabase.com/pdnupepa/?a=d&d=KHHA19220105-01.2.6

**Content Summary:**
- **REGIONAL FARMING ADVICE** - discusses which taro varieties are suited to different areas:
  - Ka'u, Hilo, Puna - suited for certain huli types (kanu maloo/dryland)
  - Waipio, Kohala Akau (North Kohala) - suited for loʻi (wetland) cultivation
- **Lists many named taro varieties** but OCR quality is heavily degraded
  - Clearly readable: "Huli-Pake" (Chinese taro variety)
  - Other variety names appear in the text but cannot be accurately transcribed due to OCR errors
  - [OCR unclear] Researchers should view original scan for accurate variety list
- Discusses dry-land vs wet-land cultivation methods
- References work by "Kauka Wailu" (likely Dr. Wilcox)

**Note:** This article contains valuable regional variety information but requires viewing the original scan to accurately extract the variety names listed.

### 3. Na Loina Kanu Kalo a na Kupuna Hawaii i ke Au Kahiko
**"The Traditions of Planting Taro of the Hawaiian Ancestors in Ancient Times"**

- **Source:** Ka Nupepa Kuokoa, Volume LXI, Number 16
- **Date:** April 21, 1922
- **URL:** https://www.papakilodatabase.com/pdnupepa/?a=d&d=KNK19220421-01.2.14

**Content Summary:**
- Traditional Hawaiian taro cultivation practices
- Two types of cultivation: loʻi (wetland) and maloo (dryland)
- Importance of letting loʻi dry out between plantings
- Adding puna keokeo (lime) to prevent rot (popo)
- Traditional soil preparation and fertilization methods
- Weeding practices (waele)
- Notes that article continues: "(Aole i pau)"

## Observed Biases and Limitations

### Time Period Bias
**All included articles are from 1900-1942.** No 1800s articles were included in these results. Possible explanations:
- 1900s saw kalo production decline, possibly prompting more explicit documentation
- 1800s articles may assume more baseline cultural knowledge
- Search/selection may have favored more readable (newer) OCR
- **Recommendation:** Future searches should specifically target 1800s materials

### Source Type Bias
**Most articles are from government/institutional authors** (Hawaii Agricultural Experiment Station, USDA). Traditional knowledge holders are underrepresented. To address:
- Search for mo'olelo (stories/narratives) about kalo
- Look for letters to editor from practitioners
- Try different newspapers that may have different editorial perspectives

### Corrections Applied (December 2024)
- Article 2: Removed hallucinated variety names; OCR too degraded for accurate extraction
- Article 5: Excluded - only mentions kalo in passing, no cultivation content

## Observations

### OCR Quality
- Historical newspaper OCR has significant errors
- Hawaiian diacriticals (ʻokina, kahakō) often missing or incorrect
- Some articles more readable than others
- 1903 and 1911 articles had better text quality than 1922 articles

### Content Patterns
- Multiple series of articles on taro cultivation (continuing articles marked "Aole i pau")
- Strong connection between Hawaii Agricultural Experiment Station and newspaper education
- Articles often written by or referencing E.V. Wilcox and other USDA agents
- Mix of traditional Hawaiian knowledge and Western agricultural science

### Regional Information Found
- **Ka'u** - dryland taro varieties
- **Hilo** - experiment station location, various cultivation methods
- **Puna** - dryland taro
- **Waipio Valley** - wetland loʻi cultivation
- **Kohala Akau (North Kohala)** - loʻi cultivation
- **Hamakua** - general cultivation advice
- **Kalihi (Oahu)** - experiment station for root rot research
- **Maui** - mentioned in Wilcox articles

## Recommendations for Researchers

1. **Start with the 1903 Sedgwick article** - clearest practical advice with specific measurements
2. **Use the 1922 KA OIHANA KANU KALO** for regional variety information
3. **Follow the Wilcox series** (1911) for comprehensive scientific approach
4. **Search by specific region + kalo** for more targeted results
5. **Note "Aole i pau" (not finished)** - indicates article series with more content in subsequent issues
